Fertility Preservation

Why Consider Egg Freezing?

A woman is born with all the eggs she will ever have. Egg quality and quantity decline with age — rapidly after 35. Egg freezing allows you to store eggs at their current quality for use in the future, when you are ready.

Who Should Consider Egg Freezing?

Women not ready for pregnancy now but wanting to preserve options, cancer patients before chemotherapy/radiation, women with a family history of early menopause, and those with rapidly declining AMH. If your AMH level is low, egg freezing earlier rather than later is especially important — read our low AMH pregnancy guide for context.

Best Age for Egg Freezing

The ideal age is 25–35. Egg quality and quantity are best in this window. After 37, yield per cycle drops significantly. However, it is still worth exploring even after 35. See our complete guide on fertility after 35 — including how many eggs to aim for per freeze cycle.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long can eggs be frozen?+

Frozen eggs can be stored for many years. Studies show good survival and pregnancy rates with eggs frozen for 5–10 years. Long-term storage is available at Mother Hospitals.

How many eggs should I freeze?+

To have a reasonable chance of one live birth, most specialists recommend freezing 10–15 mature eggs (for women under 35). Younger women may achieve this in one cycle; older women may need more cycles.

What is the egg freezing process?+

It is similar to the IVF stimulation phase: 10–12 days of hormonal stimulation, monitoring scans, egg pick-up under sedation, and immediate vitrification (fast-freezing) of mature eggs.

Is egg freezing safe?+

Egg freezing is a well-established, safe procedure. The hormonal stimulation is the same as used in standard IVF. Side effects are generally mild.

What happens when I want to use my frozen eggs?+

Your frozen eggs are thawed, fertilised with sperm via ICSI, the resulting embryos are cultured, and the best embryo is transferred to your uterus in a frozen embryo transfer (FET) cycle.
