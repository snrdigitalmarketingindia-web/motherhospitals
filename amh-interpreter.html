    Understanding AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone)

    What AMH tells you about your egg supply and fertility timeline

    AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone) is produced by the small follicles in your ovaries. It provides a reliable estimate of your remaining egg supply (ovarian reserve). Unlike other hormones, AMH can be tested on any day of the menstrual cycle and remains relatively stable month to month.

    Why AMH Matters for IVF

    AMH is the most important predictor of how many eggs will be retrieved during IVF stimulation. Women with higher AMH levels tend to respond well and produce more eggs. However, egg quality — which determines IVF success — is more strongly influenced by age than by AMH level.

    Can Low AMH Be Treated?

    There is no proven medical treatment to significantly increase AMH or create new eggs. However, DHEA supplementation (under medical guidance), optimising thyroid function, maintaining healthy weight, avoiding smoking, and antioxidant nutrients (CoQ10, Vitamin D) may help preserve existing reserve. If AMH is very low, egg freezing or embryo banking sooner rather than later is recommended.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What is a normal AMH level?

    Normal AMH ranges by age: Age 25–30: 2.5–6.3 ng/mL; Age 30–35: 1.5–4.0 ng/mL; Age 35–40: 1.0–3.5 ng/mL; Age 40–45: 0.5–2.5 ng/mL. AMH below 0.5 ng/mL at any age indicates very low ovarian reserve. These are reference ranges — individual variation exists.

    What does low AMH mean for fertility?

    Low AMH indicates fewer eggs remaining in the ovaries (diminished ovarian reserve). It does not necessarily mean you cannot conceive naturally — egg quality matters more than quantity. However, low AMH means IVF may retrieve fewer eggs and may suggest acting sooner rather than later. Egg freezing may be advised.

    What is the difference between ng/mL and pmol/L?

    ng/mL and pmol/L are two different units for reporting AMH. To convert: pmol/L ÷ 7.14 = ng/mL. For example, 14.28 pmol/L = 2.0 ng/mL. Indian labs typically report in ng/mL while some European labs use pmol/L. Our interpreter accepts both units and converts automatically.

    Can AMH levels change over time?

    AMH levels naturally decline with age and are difficult to increase significantly. However, some research suggests that nutritional status, DHEA supplementation (under medical supervision), and treating underlying conditions like PCOS or thyroid disease may stabilise levels. Lifestyle factors such as reducing smoking and maintaining healthy BMI can help preserve ovarian reserve.

    What if my AMH is very high?

    Very high AMH (above 5 ng/mL in most age groups) is often associated with PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome). Women with PCOS have many small follicles that secrete AMH continuously. High AMH with PCOS may indicate risk of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS) during IVF stimulation, requiring a modified protocol.

    Should I get an AMH test?

    An AMH test is recommended if: you are planning IVF or egg freezing, you have been trying to conceive for 6–12 months without success, you have a family history of early menopause, you have had ovarian surgery or chemotherapy, or you have irregular periods.
