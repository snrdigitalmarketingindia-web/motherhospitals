What is IVF? Who Needs It?

IVF stands for In Vitro Fertilisation — literally meaning fertilisation outside the body. In a natural conception, sperm travels through the fallopian tubes to meet and fertilise an egg. In IVF, this entire fertilisation process is carried out in a specialised laboratory, with the resulting embryo then transferred into the uterus to implant and grow into a pregnancy.

IVF is recommended when simpler fertility treatments have not worked, or when the underlying cause of infertility makes natural conception or less invasive treatment unlikely to succeed. The most common reasons couples come to Mother Hospitals for IVF include:

  • Blocked or damaged fallopian tubes: When tubes are blocked due to past infections, endometriosis, or surgery, eggs cannot travel to meet sperm — IVF bypasses the tubes entirely
  • Severe male factor infertility: Very low sperm count, poor motility, or azoospermia — often treated with ICSI alongside IVF
  • PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome): When ovulation induction and IUI have not resulted in pregnancy
  • Endometriosis: Where the condition has affected the tubes, ovaries, or embryo implantation
  • Diminished ovarian reserve / low AMH: Fewer eggs remaining, requiring careful stimulation
  • Unexplained infertility: When all standard tests come back normal but conception has not occurred after 1–2 years
  • Recurrent pregnancy loss: IVF with genetic testing (PGT) can help identify chromosomally normal embryos
  • Failed IUI cycles: After 3–4 unsuccessful IUI attempts, IVF is usually recommended

The IVF Process — Step by Step

The IVF process at Mother Hospitals follows a carefully structured six-step protocol, personalised to each patient's hormonal profile, ovarian reserve, and medical history.

Step 1: Consultation and Baseline Testing

Your IVF journey begins with a comprehensive consultation with Dr. Prashanthi. Both partners are assessed. Blood tests measure AMH, FSH, LH, oestradiol, prolactin, thyroid function, and blood glucose. A baseline transvaginal ultrasound checks antral follicle count (AFC) — the number of resting follicles in the ovaries, which predicts how well you will respond to stimulation. Semen analysis is done for the male partner. This data forms the basis of your personalised IVF protocol.

Step 2: Ovarian Stimulation (Days 1–12 approximately)

Once your menstrual cycle begins, fertility medications — typically FSH and LH hormone injections — are started to stimulate the ovaries to produce multiple follicles (each containing an egg). At Mother Hospitals, stimulation protocols are carefully calibrated to your AMH and AFC results. For eligible patients, a Needleless IVF protocol using oral medications can significantly reduce the number of injections needed. Monitoring scans are done every 2–3 days to track follicle growth and adjust medication doses as needed.

Step 3: Egg Retrieval (Ovum Pick-Up / OPU)

When follicles reach the mature size (17–20 mm), a trigger injection (hCG or GnRH agonist) is given to complete egg maturation. Egg retrieval is performed 34–36 hours later under intravenous sedation — you are comfortable and unaware during the procedure. A thin needle is guided by ultrasound through the vaginal wall into each follicle to aspirate the eggs. The procedure typically takes 15–20 minutes. Most women feel mild cramping for a few hours afterward and can return home the same day.

Step 4: Fertilisation in the Laboratory

Retrieved eggs are handed to the embryology team. In standard IVF, eggs and sperm are placed together in a culture dish. In ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection) — used for male factor infertility or previous fertilisation failure — a single sperm is injected directly into each mature egg. Fertilisation is confirmed the following day. Fertilised eggs (now called embryos) are cultured in the incubator for 3–5 days until they reach the cleavage stage (Day 3) or blastocyst stage (Day 5), the latter being the preferred stage for transfer as it has higher implantation potential.

Step 5: Embryo Transfer

On Day 3 or Day 5 after egg retrieval, one or two best-quality embryos are selected and transferred into the uterus through a thin, flexible catheter passed through the cervix. No anaesthesia is needed — most patients describe the sensation as similar to a smear test. You rest for 30 minutes afterward and are then free to go home. The remaining good-quality embryos are frozen (vitrified) for future use if needed.

Step 6: The Two-Week Wait and Pregnancy Test

The 14 days following embryo transfer — often called the "two-week wait" — are emotionally the most demanding part of the IVF cycle. During this time, a progesterone support medication is continued. On Day 14, a blood beta-hCG test is done to confirm whether implantation has occurred. A positive result is then confirmed with an ultrasound scan at 6–7 weeks to detect the foetal heartbeat.

IVF Success Rates by Age at Mother Hospitals

Honest, age-stratified success rates are one of the first things couples ask about — and rightly so. Here are the realistic benchmarks at Mother Hospitals, based on live birth rates per transfer cycle:

  • Under 35 years: 50–65% success rate per transfer — the best outcomes, especially for women with good ovarian reserve, normal uterine anatomy, and no major male factor issues
  • 35–37 years: 40–50% per transfer — strong results, though age-related changes in egg quality begin to appear
  • 38–40 years: 30–40% per transfer — viable outcomes, often optimised with ERA Test and blastocyst transfer
  • 41–42 years: 20–30% with own eggs; Donor Egg IVF at this stage can restore success rates to 50–60%+

Dr. Prashanthi's perspective: "Age is one factor — but it is not the only factor. I have seen 39-year-old women with excellent ovarian reserve achieve pregnancy on the first cycle, and 30-year-old women with severe PCOS and a thin endometrium requiring multiple attempts. Every case is different. My job is to give you an honest picture of your specific situation, not a generic statistic."

Frequently Asked Questions

What is IVF treatment and who needs it?

IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation) is a fertility treatment where eggs are fertilised with sperm outside the body in a laboratory, and the resulting embryo is transferred into the uterus. It is recommended for blocked fallopian tubes, severe male infertility, PCOS unresponsive to simpler treatments, unexplained infertility, low ovarian reserve, endometriosis, and failed IUI cycles.

How many steps are in the IVF process?

The IVF process has six main steps: (1) Consultation and baseline testing, (2) Ovarian stimulation with hormone medications for 10–14 days, (3) Egg retrieval under sedation, (4) Fertilisation in the embryology laboratory, (5) Embryo transfer into the uterus on Day 3 or Day 5, and (6) Pregnancy blood test 14 days after transfer.
