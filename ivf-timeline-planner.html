Understanding IVF Protocols

What each protocol means and who it is best for

The IVF protocol is the plan for using fertility medications to stimulate the ovaries, prevent premature ovulation, trigger egg maturation, and prepare the uterus for embryo transfer. The choice of protocol is tailored to each patient based on age, AMH, antral follicle count, and previous IVF history.

Antagonist Protocol — The Most Common

The antagonist protocol is the standard first-line IVF protocol. Stimulation injections start on Day 2-3. An antagonist injection (Cetrorelix or Ganirelix) is added from Day 5-7 to prevent premature ovulation. Total stimulation: 10-12 days. Preferred for PCOS patients, normal responders, and women at OHSS risk. Mother Hospitals uses this protocol for most patients.

Freeze-All Strategy

In a freeze-all cycle, all embryos are frozen after retrieval rather than performing a fresh transfer. The frozen embryo transfer (FET) occurs in a subsequent prepared cycle. Freeze-all is preferred for women at OHSS risk, those having PGT-A testing, when progesterone rises prematurely, or when the endometrial lining is suboptimal. FET outcomes are generally comparable to fresh transfer.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does an IVF cycle take from start to finish?

A standard antagonist IVF cycle takes approximately 14-17 days from Day 1 of period to embryo transfer. Including the wait for a pregnancy test, a full cycle is approximately 4-5 weeks. A frozen embryo transfer (FET) cycle adds a preparation phase of 2-4 weeks after the egg retrieval cycle.

What is the difference between antagonist and long agonist protocols?

Antagonist protocol: Stimulation starts Day 2-3, antagonist added Day 5-6 onwards, shorter (12-14 days), preferred for most patients especially those at OHSS risk (PCOS). Long agonist protocol: Starts with down-regulation in the previous cycle (2-4 weeks), then stimulation. More controlled but longer. Preferred for poor responders or patients with endometriosis.

What is a blastocyst transfer?

A blastocyst is an embryo cultured to Day 5 in the laboratory, with about 100-200 cells. Blastocyst transfer has higher implantation rates than Day 3 transfer because only the strongest embryos survive to blastocyst stage. Mother Hospitals prefers blastocyst transfer when sufficient embryos are available.

What is PGT-A (preimplantation genetic testing)?

PGT-A tests embryos for chromosomal abnormalities before transfer. A biopsy is taken from blastocyst stage embryos. Normal (euploid) embryos are frozen and transferred in a subsequent FET cycle. PGT-A significantly improves IVF success rates in women over 37 and those with recurrent implantation failure.

What happens at the egg retrieval?

Egg retrieval (oocyte pick-up / OPU) is performed under sedation. Using transvaginal ultrasound guidance, a thin needle is inserted into each follicle to aspirate the egg. The procedure takes 15-30 minutes. Rest for 24 hours is recommended. Mild cramping and bloating are normal.

How long after retrieval is the embryo transfer?

For a fresh blastocyst transfer: 5-6 days after egg retrieval. For a fresh Day 3 transfer: 3 days after retrieval. For a freeze-all strategy: embryos are frozen after retrieval, and transfer occurs in a subsequent prepared FET cycle, typically 1-2 months later. Freeze-all is preferred for OHSS risk or PGT-A testing.
