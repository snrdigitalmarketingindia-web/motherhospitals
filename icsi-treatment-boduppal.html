🔬 What is ICSI?

A single healthy sperm is selected under high magnification and injected directly into the egg using a fine needle. Bypasses all natural fertilisation barriers — ideal when sperm cannot reach or penetrate the egg naturally.

📉 Who Needs ICSI?

Low sperm count (< 5 million). Poor motility (< 20% progressive). High morphology defects. Previous IVF with low fertilisation. TESA sperm (retrieved from testis). Anti-sperm antibodies. Unexplained fertilisation failure.

📊 Fertilisation Rate

ICSI achieves 70–80% fertilisation per mature egg — significantly higher than conventional IVF insemination (60–70%). More fertilised eggs = more embryos = more chances. Nearly all IVF cycles at Mother Hospitals include ICSI.

⚗️ TESA + ICSI for Zero Sperm

No sperm in semen (azoospermia)? TESA (Testicular Sperm Aspiration) retrieves sperm directly from the testis under local anaesthesia. Retrieved sperm used for ICSI same day. 30–50% of non-obstructive azoospermia cases yield usable sperm.

🏆 PICSI — Best Sperm Selection

PICSI selects sperm based on hyaluronic acid binding — a marker of genetic maturity. Better sperm quality → better embryos → less miscarriage. Recommended when sperm DNA fragmentation is high.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is ICSI better than regular IVF?
ICSI is not "better" — it's more appropriate for certain cases. When sperm quality is good, conventional IVF is fine. When there's male factor, previous fertilisation failure, or TESA sperm — ICSI is the right choice. Dr. Prashanthi recommends ICSI in most cases as it gives the best fertilisation outcomes.
Does ICSI guarantee pregnancy?
No. ICSI guarantees higher fertilisation rates — but implantation (embryo attaching to uterus) depends on embryo quality, uterine lining, and other factors. 70–80% fertilisation doesn't mean 70–80% pregnancy — it means you'll have good-quality embryos to transfer.
Is ICSI safe for the baby?
Decades of data on ICSI babies show no increased risk of birth defects compared to natural conception. Slight increased risk for male-factor patients (Y-chromosome microdeletion) — but this is from the underlying condition, not ICSI itself. Dr. Prashanthi discusses this clearly.
How long does the ICSI process take?
ICSI is part of the IVF cycle: stimulation (10–14 days) → egg retrieval (Day 14–16) → ICSI performed same day → embryo culture Day 1–5 → transfer Day 3 or Day 5. Total cycle: 3–4 weeks from period start to pregnancy test.
