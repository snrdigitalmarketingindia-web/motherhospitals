Laparoscopic Procedures at Mother Hospitals

🔴 Fibroid Removal (Myomectomy)

Laparoscopic removal of uterine fibroids — preserving the uterus. Suitable for single or multiple fibroids. Shorter recovery than open myomectomy. Fertility preserved.

🟣 Endometriosis Surgery

Laparoscopic ablation or excision of endometriotic deposits. Removal of endometrioma (chocolate cysts). Adhesiolysis. Improves natural conception rates and reduces chronic pelvic pain.

🟢 Ovarian Cyst Removal (Cystectomy)

Laparoscopic cyst removal — dermoid cysts, functional cysts, endometriomas, haemorrhagic cysts. Ovarian tissue preserved where possible. Day procedure.

💊 PCOS Ovarian Drilling

For clomiphene-resistant PCOS — laparoscopic diathermy of ovarian surface restores natural ovulation in ~80% of cases. Avoids OHSS risk of IVF stimulation in this group.

🔵 Tubal Surgery

Salpingostomy for hydrosalpinx (blocked/dilated tubes), tubal ligation reversal, salpingectomy before IVF (hydrosalpinx removal improves IVF outcomes by 25–30%).

🔬 Diagnostic Laparoscopy

Gold-standard investigation for unexplained infertility, pelvic pain, suspected endometriosis — direct visualisation of pelvic organs. Chromopertubation to check tubal patency.

Laparoscopy FAQs

Is laparoscopy safe for future fertility?
Yes — laparoscopy is the fertility-preserving approach for most gynaecological conditions. Fibroid myomectomy, ovarian cystectomy, endometriosis excision, and PCOS ovarian drilling are all performed with the explicit goal of preserving or improving fertility.
How long does laparoscopic surgery take?
Diagnostic laparoscopy: 30–45 minutes. Ovarian cystectomy: 45–60 minutes. Fibroid myomectomy: 60–90 minutes. Endometriosis surgery: 60–120 minutes depending on extent. Most patients go home 4–6 hours after surgery.
When can I try to conceive after laparoscopy?
For ovarian drilling or diagnostic laparoscopy: next cycle or 4–6 weeks. For myomectomy: 3–6 months depending on fibroid size and depth. For endometriosis surgery: 3 months. Dr. Prashanthi Reddy will give you a specific timeline based on your procedure.
Do I need general anaesthesia for laparoscopy?
Yes — laparoscopy requires general anaesthesia because pneumoperitoneum (CO2 gas to inflate the abdomen) is needed. The anaesthesia is short-acting; most patients are awake and alert within 1–2 hours after surgery.
