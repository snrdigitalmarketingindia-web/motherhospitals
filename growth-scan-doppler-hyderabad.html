Third Trimester Monitoring

What is a Growth Scan?

A growth scan is an ultrasound performed from 28 weeks onwards to check how well your baby is growing inside the womb. Unlike earlier scans that look for structural abnormalities, the growth scan focuses on measuring your baby's size, estimating weight, and checking amniotic fluid — giving a clear picture of fetal wellbeing in the later stages of pregnancy.

What Does the Growth Scan Measure?

The growth scan uses standard measurements to estimate your baby's size and growth rate:

BPD (Biparietal Diameter) — width of the baby's head
HC (Head Circumference) — measurement around the head
AC (Abdominal Circumference) — measurement around the tummy
FL (Femur Length) — length of the thigh bone
EFW (Estimated Fetal Weight) — calculated from above measurements
AFI or SDP — amniotic fluid level
Placental position — location and maturity
Fetal presentation — head down, breech, or transverse

Growth Scan vs Anomaly Scan — What's the Difference?

Many women confuse the growth scan with the anomaly scan. They are very different:

When done18–22 weeks28 weeks onwards
PurposeCheck baby's organs & structureCheck growth rate & wellbeing

Fetal Blood Flow Monitoring

What is a Doppler Scan?

A Doppler scan is a special type of ultrasound that measures the speed and direction of blood flow through blood vessels — in the umbilical cord, the fetal brain, and other fetal vessels. It is the most accurate non-invasive way to assess whether the placenta is delivering enough oxygen and nutrients to your baby.

What Does Doppler Measure?

Umbilical Artery (UA) Doppler

The most important Doppler measurement. It checks resistance to blood flow between the placenta and baby. Abnormal UA Doppler (absent or reversed end-diastolic flow) is a sign of severe placental insufficiency and may indicate the baby needs early delivery.

Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Doppler

Measures blood flow to the baby's brain. In IUGR, the baby compensates by increasing blood flow to the brain (brain-sparing effect) — this shows as reduced resistance on MCA Doppler and indicates the baby is under stress.

Ductus Venosus (DV) Doppler

A more advanced measurement used when UA Doppler is severely abnormal. Ductus venosus Doppler reflects venous blood flow to the fetal heart. Absent or reversed 'a-wave' in DV Doppler is an indication for urgent delivery.

When is Doppler Needed?

Doppler scan is routinely added to growth scans in the following situations:

IUGR (Growth Restriction)

When the baby measures small on growth scan (below 10th centile), Doppler assesses whether blood flow is compromised.

High Blood Pressure / Pre-eclampsia

Hypertension can impair placental function. Doppler monitors this closely.

Twin Pregnancies

Twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome (TTTS) and selective IUGR in twins require regular Doppler surveillance.

Gestational Diabetes

Poorly controlled diabetes can affect fetal growth and placental blood flow.

Reduced Fetal Movements

When a mother notices less baby movement, Doppler + biophysical profile is used to assess fetal condition urgently.

IVF Pregnancies

IVF pregnancies carry a slightly higher risk of placental insufficiency and growth restriction — regular growth + Doppler monitoring is recommended.

Interpreting Your Scan

Understanding Growth Scan Results

Growth scan reports can be confusing. Here is what the key measurements and terms mean, explained clearly.

Estimated Fetal Weight (EFW) — Centiles Explained

EFW is plotted on a centile chart against the normal range for that gestational week. This tells you where your baby falls compared to other babies of the same age:

50th Centile

The average weight for the gestational week. Exactly half of babies weigh more, half weigh less. This is the ideal mid-point.

10th–90th Centile

Normal range. Your baby's weight falls within the expected range for their age. No action typically needed if growth velocity is maintained.

Below 10th Centile

Small for gestational age (SGA). May indicate IUGR or constitutional smallness. Requires Doppler and further monitoring to assess cause.

Below 3rd Centile

Severe growth restriction. Requires urgent specialist review, Doppler assessment, and decision about timing of delivery.

What is IUGR (Intrauterine Growth Restriction)?

IUGR (Intrauterine Growth Restriction) means the baby is not growing at the expected rate. It is different from a naturally small baby — IUGR usually indicates a problem with placental blood supply. There are two types:

Early-onset IUGR (before 32 weeks)

Usually caused by placental insufficiency or chromosomal problems. More severe and requires intensive monitoring with Doppler. Risk of premature delivery.

Late-onset IUGR (after 32 weeks)

More common. Often associated with hypertension or placental aging. Doppler changes may be subtle. Regular growth scans every 2 weeks with close monitoring.

Amniotic Fluid Level (AFI and SDP)

Amniotic fluid is the liquid surrounding your baby. Growth scans measure this in two ways:

AFI (Amniotic Fluid Index)

Total fluid measurement across 4 quadrants. Normal range: 8–24 cm. AFI below 5 (oligohydramnios) or above 24 (polyhydramnios) needs investigation.

SDP (Single Deepest Pocket)

Measurement of the deepest fluid pocket. Normal: 2–8 cm. Now preferred over AFI in many guidelines for accuracy in predicting poor outcomes.

Biophysical Profile Score

The Biophysical Profile (BPP) combines ultrasound observations with a cardiotocography (CTG) reading to score your baby's wellbeing out of 10. Each of 5 parameters scores 0 or 2 points:

ParameterScore 2Score 0
Fetal Breathing Movements≥1 episode of 30 sec in 30 minAbsent
Fetal Movements≥3 discrete body movementsFewer than 3
Fetal ToneActive extension & flexionNo movement
Amniotic FluidSDP ≥2 cmSDP <2 cm
CTG (Non-stress test)Reactive (≥2 accelerations)Non-reactive

Score 8–10: Normal. Score 6: Borderline — repeat in 24 hours. Score ≤4: Abnormal — urgent management needed.

Doppler Interpretation

Understanding Doppler Results

Doppler results use waveform patterns to describe blood flow. Your doctor will explain what these mean for your specific pregnancy.

Normal Doppler — What It Means

In a normal Doppler result:

Umbilical artery shows continuous forward flow throughout the cardiac cycle
Pulsatility Index (PI) and Resistance Index (RI) are within normal range for gestational age
Middle cerebral artery PI is higher than umbilical artery (no brain-sparing)
Ductus venosus shows normal forward 'a-wave'

Normal Doppler in an IUGR baby is reassuring — it means the placenta is still coping adequately and immediate delivery is not required.

Absent or Reversed End-Diastolic Flow — Emergency Signs

This is the most serious Doppler finding:

Absent end-diastolic flow (AEDF) — blood stops flowing during the relaxation phase. Indicates severe placental resistance. Baby needs very close monitoring (usually hospital admission).
Reversed end-diastolic flow (REDF) — blood flows backwards. This is an obstetric emergency. Immediate senior review required; delivery usually indicated within 24–48 hours.
Absent 'a-wave' in ductus venosus — indicates cardiac compromise. Emergency delivery is usually required.

If you have been told your Doppler is severely abnormal, do not leave hospital without a clear management plan. Call 97059 93366 immediately.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How often do I need growth scans?+

In a normal low-risk pregnancy, growth scans are typically done at 28, 32, and 36 weeks. In high-risk pregnancies (IUGR, hypertension, twins, diabetes), growth scans may be done every 2–4 weeks from 28 weeks. Your doctor at Mother Hospitals will recommend the right schedule for you based on your specific risk factors.

Is Doppler scan safe for baby?+

Yes, completely. Doppler ultrasound uses the same safe sound waves as standard pregnancy ultrasound — there is no radiation involved. It has been used safely in obstetric practice for decades and is recommended by international guidelines for high-risk pregnancy monitoring. There are no known harmful effects to the mother or baby.

What if my baby is small on growth scan?+

A small measurement on growth scan does not automatically mean something is wrong. Some babies are constitutionally small (genetically small parents, for example). What matters is the growth velocity (whether the baby is continuing to grow) and the Doppler findings. If Doppler is normal and growth is continuing — even below the 10th centile — close monitoring may be all that is needed. If Doppler is abnormal, further intervention may be required. Dr. Prashanthi will discuss the specific findings and plan with you.

Can growth scan detect all problems?+

Growth scan is very good at detecting growth restriction, fluid abnormalities, and placental issues, but it cannot detect all fetal problems. Structural abnormalities are better assessed at the anomaly scan (18–22 weeks). Chromosomal conditions are screened for separately. Growth scan is one component of overall antenatal monitoring — it works best alongside Doppler, CTG, and clinical assessment.

What is a normal EFW at 32 weeks?+

At 32 weeks, the average estimated fetal weight (50th centile) is approximately 1.7–1.8 kg (1,700–1,800 grams). The normal range at 32 weeks is roughly 1,300–2,200 grams (10th to 90th centile). Your scan report will show the centile for your baby's specific measurements. A single weight estimate is less important than whether the baby has been growing consistently over time.

What do reduced fetal movements mean?+

Reduced fetal movements (RFM) — fewer or weaker movements than usual — can be a sign that the baby is under stress and needs assessment. Any episode of reduced movements should be reported to your doctor or hospital the same day — do not wait. At Mother Hospitals, we assess RFM with a growth scan, Doppler, and CTG on the same visit. Do not hesitate to call 97059 93366 if you notice less movement.

Can I eat before a growth scan?+

Yes — you can eat and drink normally before a growth scan. Unlike early pregnancy scans (dating scan) which may require a full bladder, third-trimester growth scans do not require any special preparation regarding food or drink. Eating before the scan may actually increase fetal activity, making the scan easier to perform.

Do I need a full bladder for growth scan?+

No. Growth scans in the third trimester are performed through the abdomen (transabdominal ultrasound) and do not require a full bladder. By 28 weeks, there is enough amniotic fluid to visualise the baby clearly without a full bladder. You may be asked to empty your bladder before the scan for comfort.
