Understanding Your Menstrual Cycle

What's happening in your body across the four phases

The menstrual cycle is a monthly hormonal process that prepares the body for pregnancy. A typical cycle is 28 days, but anywhere from 21 to 35 days is considered normal. Tracking your cycle helps you understand your body, identify irregularities early, and plan or avoid pregnancy.

When to Worry About Irregular Periods

Cycles that vary by more than 7 days, periods absent for 3+ months, extremely heavy or painful periods, or spotting between cycles may indicate PCOS, thyroid disorders, endometriosis, or premature ovarian insufficiency — all treatable with early diagnosis.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is this period tracker?

This tracker is most accurate for women with regular cycles (21–35 days). It uses the standard LMP (last menstrual period) method. If your cycles are irregular (vary by more than 7 days), predictions may be less precise — please consult Dr. Prashanthi Reddy for personalised guidance.

What is a normal menstrual cycle length?

A normal cycle is 21–35 days. Cycles shorter than 21 days or longer than 35 days consistently may indicate PCOS, thyroid issues, high prolactin, or premature ovarian insufficiency. If your cycles are irregular, a hormonal blood panel (FSH, LH, AMH, TSH) and pelvic ultrasound is recommended.

When is my most fertile time?

Your fertile window spans 6 days — the 5 days before ovulation and the day of ovulation itself. For a 28-day cycle, ovulation occurs around Day 14, making Days 9–14 the most fertile. Sperm can survive 5 days in the reproductive tract, so intercourse in the days before ovulation is just as important as on ovulation day.

Why is my period irregular?

Common causes: PCOS (most common), thyroid disorders, high prolactin (hyperprolactinaemia), extreme weight changes, excessive exercise, stress, perimenopause, and premature ovarian insufficiency (POI). If cycles vary by more than 7 days or you skip periods, a hormonal panel and ultrasound at Mother Hospitals is recommended.

What is the luteal phase and why does it matter?

The luteal phase is the post-ovulation phase (Days 15–28). Progesterone rises to prepare the uterine lining for implantation. A luteal phase shorter than 10 days (luteal phase defect) can prevent embryo implantation and cause recurrent early miscarriages. Diagnosed with a Day 21 progesterone blood test.

When should I see a doctor about my periods?

See a gynaecologist if: periods are absent for 3+ months, cycles are consistently shorter than 21 or longer than 35 days, bleeding is very heavy, you have severe pelvic pain, or you have been trying to conceive for 6–12 months without success. Mother Hospitals: 97059 93366.
