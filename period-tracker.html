🔄 Your 4 Cycle Phases

Menstrual (Days 1–5): Uterine lining sheds. Rest and hydrate.
Follicular (Days 1–13): Follicles mature. Oestrogen rises. Energy increases.
Ovulation (~Day 14): Egg released. Peak fertility. Best time to conceive.
Luteal (Days 15–28): Progesterone rises. PMS symptoms may appear.

Understanding Your Menstrual Cycle

What's happening in your body across the four phases

The menstrual cycle is a monthly hormonal process that prepares the body for pregnancy. A typical cycle is 28 days, but anywhere from 21 to 35 days is considered normal. Tracking your cycle helps you understand your body, identify irregularities early, and plan or avoid pregnancy.

When to Worry About Irregular Periods

Cycles that vary by more than 7 days, periods absent for 3+ months, extremely heavy or painful periods, or spotting between cycles may indicate PCOS, thyroid disorders, endometriosis, or premature ovarian insufficiency — all treatable with early diagnosis.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is this period tracker?

This tracker is most accurate for women with regular cycles (21–35 days). It uses the standard LMP method. If your cycles are irregular (vary by more than 7 days), predictions may be less precise — consult Dr. E. Prashanthi Reddy for personalised guidance.

What is a normal menstrual cycle length?

A normal cycle is 21–35 days. Cycles shorter than 21 or longer than 35 days may indicate PCOS, thyroid issues, or premature ovarian insufficiency. A hormonal blood panel and ultrasound is recommended.

When is my most fertile time?

Your fertile window spans 6 days — the 5 days before ovulation and the day of ovulation. For a 28-day cycle, this is Days 9–14. Sperm survive 5 days, so intercourse before ovulation matters as much as on the day itself.

Why is my period irregular?

Common causes: PCOS, thyroid disorders, high prolactin, weight changes, stress, perimenopause, and POI. If cycles vary by more than 7 days or you skip periods, a hormonal panel at Mother Hospitals is recommended.

What is the luteal phase and why does it matter?

The luteal phase (Days 15–28) is post-ovulation. Progesterone rises for implantation. A luteal phase shorter than 10 days can cause recurrent miscarriage. Diagnosed with a Day 21 progesterone test.

When should I see a doctor about my periods?

See a doctor if periods are absent 3+ months, cycles are consistently <21 or >35 days, bleeding is very heavy, pain is severe, or you've been trying to conceive for 6–12 months without success. Call: 97059 93366.
