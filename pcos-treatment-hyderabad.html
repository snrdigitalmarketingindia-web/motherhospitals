About PCOS

What is PCOS / PCOD?

PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) is a hormonal imbalance where the ovaries produce excess androgens (male hormones), causing irregular periods, cysts on the ovaries, and ovulation problems. It affects 1 in 5 women in India.

PCOS Symptoms

Irregular or missed periods, excessive hair growth (hirsutism), acne, weight gain, hair loss, difficulty getting pregnant, and multiple small cysts visible on ultrasound.

PCOS & Fertility

PCOS can cause anovulation (lack of ovulation), making natural pregnancy difficult. However, with proper treatment — from ovulation induction tablets to IVF treatment in Hyderabad — most women with PCOS can achieve pregnancy. Read: can PCOS patients get pregnant?

Step-by-Step Treatment

The PCOS Treatment Ladder

We always start with the simplest, least invasive approach and only advance when needed. Most PCOS patients achieve pregnancy at Steps 1–3 without requiring IVF.

1

Lifestyle Modification & Metformin

Start Here

A 5–10% weight reduction restores spontaneous ovulation in 50–80% of overweight PCOS women — without any tablets. Low-GI diet + 150 min/week exercise is the foundation. Metformin 500–1500mg/day reduces insulin resistance, lowers androgens, and improves ovulation. Give this 3–6 months before advancing.

2

Ovulation Induction — Letrozole (First Line)

Most Common

Letrozole 2.5–7.5mg, Days 2–6 of the cycle. Follicular monitoring ultrasound on Days 10–12. Trigger injection when the follicle reaches 18–20mm. Timed intercourse or IUI. Success rate: 15–25% per cycle. Letrozole is preferred over Clomiphene for PCOS as it causes fewer side effects and better ovulation rates. Up to 6 cycles attempted.

3

IUI with Low-Dose Gonadotrophins

If OI Fails

Low-dose injectable gonadotrophins (FSH) combined with IUI (Intrauterine Insemination). Careful monitoring is essential to avoid multi-follicular response and OHSS in PCOS. Success rate: 15–20% per IUI cycle. Usually 2–3 IUI cycles before proceeding to IVF. Learn about IUI at Mother Hospitals →

4

IVF with PCOS-Safe Protocol + Freeze-All

Highest Success

GnRH antagonist protocol with careful dose calibration. GnRH agonist trigger (not hCG) to prevent OHSS. All embryos frozen — no fresh transfer in PCOS IVF. Frozen Embryo Transfer (FET) 4–6 weeks later. Success rate: 65–75% (under 35 with PCOS). PCOS patients actually have some of the highest IVF success rates due to good egg numbers and young egg quality. PCOS IVF success rates →

IVF for PCOS

The Freeze-All Strategy — Why PCOS Needs It

At Mother Hospitals, all PCOS patients undergoing IVF follow a Freeze-All protocol. Here's why this makes IVF safer and more effective for PCOS.

❄️ What is Freeze-All?

In a Freeze-All cycle, all embryos are vitrified (frozen) after egg retrieval and ICSI. No embryo is transferred during the stimulated cycle. Transfer happens in a separate, unstimulated Frozen Embryo Transfer (FET) cycle 4–6 weeks later.

🛡️ Why PCOS Needs Freeze-All

PCOS patients produce many eggs in IVF — which is good, but it also means high OHSS risk in a fresh transfer cycle. A fresh hCG trigger + fresh transfer in a hyperstimulated uterus causes higher OHSS risk and lower implantation rates. Freeze-All eliminates both problems.

📈 Better Implantation in FET

Studies show frozen-thawed embryo transfers in PCOS patients have 20–30% higher implantation rates compared to fresh transfers. The endometrium in a natural FET cycle is more receptive than in a hormonally stimulated fresh cycle. Best quality blastocyst is chosen for first transfer.

🔄 Remaining Embryos Available

Because PCOS produces multiple eggs, you often have several good-quality embryos vitrified from one retrieval. If the first FET doesn't result in pregnancy, additional embryos can be transferred in subsequent cycles — without repeating the full egg retrieval process.

Safety in IVF

OHSS Prevention — Protecting You During IVF

Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome (OHSS) is the main IVF risk for PCOS patients. At Mother Hospitals, we use a multi-layered prevention protocol so that OHSS is extremely rare.

🔬 GnRH Antagonist Protocol

We use the antagonist protocol for all PCOS IVF — not the older long agonist protocol. The antagonist approach allows us to switch the trigger to a GnRH agonist (Lupron trigger) instead of hCG, which dramatically reduces OHSS risk. The antagonist also allows precise control of stimulation duration.

💉 GnRH Agonist Trigger (Not hCG)

The standard hCG trigger carries significant OHSS risk in PCOS. We use a GnRH agonist trigger (e.g., Leuprolide acetate) instead. This causes a shorter LH surge, triggers ovulation safely, and reduces OHSS risk by over 80% compared to hCG — while maintaining the same egg maturation rates.

❄️ Freeze-All Policy

We never perform a fresh embryo transfer in PCOS IVF cycles at Mother Hospitals. The Freeze-All + FET approach eliminates the risk of OHSS being worsened by an ongoing hCG-producing early pregnancy (which is the most severe and prolonged form of OHSS). This is a non-negotiable safety policy for PCOS.

📊 Careful Dose Calibration

Stimulation starts at the lowest effective dose, based on your AMH and antral follicle count. For high-reserve PCOS patients (AMH >5 ng/mL), we use particularly gentle stimulation to avoid recruiting too many follicles. Serial ultrasound monitoring every 2–3 days allows real-time dose adjustment.

💊 Cabergoline Prophylaxis

In high-risk cycles where AMH is very high or many follicles develop, we may add Cabergoline 0.5mg/day for 8 days post-trigger. This dopamine agonist has been shown to reduce moderate OHSS by blocking VEGF-mediated vascular permeability — a key mechanism in OHSS development.

🧃 Post-Retrieval Monitoring

All PCOS patients are monitored for 5–7 days after egg retrieval for early OHSS signs (bloating, abdominal pain, nausea). High fluid intake, high-protein diet, and rest are advised. If mild OHSS develops, it resolves within 1–2 weeks without transfer — which has been safely deferred to the FET cycle.

Important: Pregnancy Safety

PCOS & Miscarriage Risk — What You Need to Know

PCOS is associated with a higher miscarriage rate. Understanding why — and what we do about it — is important for every PCOS patient planning pregnancy.

Why PCOS Increases Miscarriage Risk

Women with PCOS have approximately 40–60% higher miscarriage rate compared to the general population. Reasons include:

• Elevated LH levels — cause premature egg maturation and abnormal early development
• Insulin resistance — impairs endometrial development and implantation quality
• Androgen excess — negatively affects egg quality
• Anovulatory cycles — when ovulation does occur, it may be suboptimal
• Endometrial abnormalities — impaired uterine receptivity in some PCOS patients

PCOS + Pregnancy After Conception

Once a PCOS pregnancy is confirmed, there is elevated risk of:

• Gestational diabetes — 2–3× higher risk; glucose tolerance test at 24–28 weeks is essential
• Pregnancy-induced hypertension / preeclampsia — monitor BP from 20 weeks
• Preterm birth — slightly elevated risk; regular monitoring advised
• Large-for-gestational-age baby — if blood sugar is not well-controlled

Dr. Prashanthi Reddy coordinates with your obstetrician to ensure safe PCOS pregnancy monitoring.

How We Reduce PCOS Miscarriage Risk

Our miscarriage prevention protocol for PCOS patients:

GnRH agonist trigger in IVF — avoids hCG excess which elevates LH and impairs egg maturation
Freeze-All + FET — transfers in a calm, unstimulated endometrium improves implantation quality and reduces early pregnancy loss
Metformin continuation through early pregnancy — reduces miscarriage rate in PCOS by improving insulin sensitivity. Evidence supports continuation to at least 12 weeks
Progesterone support from transfer — vaginal progesterone continued to 12 weeks gestation to support corpus luteum function
Early pregnancy monitoring — viability scan at 6–7 weeks, follow-up at 8–9 weeks to confirm ongoing pregnancy before discharge to obstetric care
Blood sugar optimisation before IVF — fasting glucose and insulin measured; dietary intervention or Metformin started to normalise glucose before egg retrieval
Nutrition & Lifestyle

PCOS Diet & Lifestyle Guide

Diet and lifestyle are the most powerful tools in PCOS management — often more effective than medication for symptom control and fertility restoration.

✅ Foods to Eat (Low GI)

  • 🥦 All vegetables (especially leafy greens)
  • 🫘 Legumes — lentils, rajma, chickpeas
  • 🌾 Whole grains — brown rice, oats, millets
  • 🥚 Eggs & lean protein (fish, chicken)
  • 🥑 Healthy fats — nuts, seeds, avocado
  • 🍓 Low-sugar fruits — berries, guava, apple
  • 🧀 Full-fat dairy (in moderation)
  • 💧 Water, herbal teas, green tea

❌ Foods to Reduce / Avoid

  • 🍚 White rice, white bread, maida products
  • 🍬 Sugar, sweets, mithai, desserts
  • 🥤 Sugary drinks — juice, soda, energy drinks
  • 🍟 Deep-fried foods and fast food
  • 🧁 Bakery products (high GI)
  • 🥛 Low-fat flavoured dairy (high sugar)
  • 🍺 Alcohol (raises cortisol, worsens IR)
  • ☕ Excess caffeine (>200mg/day during TTC)

🏃 Exercise for PCOS

The best exercise combination for PCOS:

HIIT (High Intensity Interval Training) — most effective at reducing insulin resistance in PCOS. 20–30 min, 3×/week.

Resistance training — builds muscle which increases insulin sensitivity. 2–3 sessions/week.

Daily walking — 30–45 minutes even improves fasting glucose. Start here if deconditioned.

Avoid extreme over-exercising — it raises cortisol and can worsen hormonal imbalance.

💊 Supplements for PCOS

Myo-inositol + D-chiro-inositol (40:1 ratio) — improves insulin sensitivity, reduces testosterone, restores ovulation in PCOS. Strong evidence. 4g myo-inositol + 100mg D-chiro-inositol/day.

Vitamin D — often low in PCOS; supplementation improves insulin sensitivity and menstrual regularity.

Omega-3 — reduces inflammation and testosterone.

Folic acid — 400–800mcg/day essential when trying to conceive.

Always discuss supplements with Dr. Prashanthi Reddy before starting.

The Bottom Line: How Much Can Lifestyle Help?

Research consistently shows: 5–10% weight loss restores regular periods in 50–80% of overweight PCOS women. 30% of PCOS patients who make lifestyle changes conceive within 6 months without any fertility medication. Lifestyle is not a "nice to have" — it is the most effective fertility treatment for PCOS when overweight is a factor. Even if you need IVF, better metabolic health improves egg quality, endometrial receptivity, and reduces miscarriage risk.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is PCOS — and why is it now called PMOS?+

Short answer: PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) was officially renamed PMOS — Polycystic Ovarian Morphology Syndrome by the Endocrine Society in May 2026. The new name better describes the condition as ovarian morphology rather than a "syndrome". The treatment, symptoms, and management remain the same. Both names are currently used.

Can I get pregnant naturally with PCOS / PMOS?+

Short answer: Yes — many women with PCOS conceive naturally. Detailed answer: PCOS affects ovulation. With lifestyle changes (diet, weight management), many women resume regular cycles and conceive without fertility treatment. For those who need help, ovulation induction tablets (Letrozole) are the first step. Dr. Prashanthi Reddy always starts with the least invasive approach.

Does PCOS affect IVF success rates?+

Short answer: Women with PCOS generally have good IVF outcomes. Detailed answer: PCOS patients often have many follicles and produce multiple eggs in IVF, which is an advantage. The main risk is OHSS (ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome). At Mother Hospitals, we use modified protocols, trigger shot adjustments, and freeze-all strategies to prevent OHSS and maximise safety.

What is the treatment protocol for PCOS at Mother Hospitals?+

Dr. Prashanthi Reddy follows a step-up protocol: (1) Lifestyle modification + diet (low-GI, anti-inflammatory) (2) Metformin for insulin resistance (3) Ovulation induction with Letrozole or Clomiphene (4) IUI if ovulation induction alone doesn't achieve pregnancy (5) IVF with ICSI for resistant cases. Treatment is always tailored to your individual goals.

What medications are used for PCOS treatment?+

Short answer: Metformin, Letrozole, Clomiphene, and hormonal medications. Common medications include: Metformin (reduces insulin resistance), Letrozole or Clomiphene Citrate (induce ovulation), progesterone (regulate periods), and hormonal contraceptives (to manage symptoms when pregnancy is not the goal).

Is PCOS curable permanently?+

Short answer: PCOS cannot be permanently cured, but it is very manageable. Detailed answer: PCOS is a lifelong hormonal condition, but its symptoms — irregular periods, weight gain, excess hair — can be significantly controlled with lifestyle changes and medication. Most women with PCOS can regulate their cycles, manage symptoms effectively, and achieve pregnancy with the right treatment.

What is the best diet for PCOS / PMOS?+

Short answer: A low-glycaemic, high-protein, anti-inflammatory diet. What to eat: Vegetables, whole grains, legumes, lean protein (eggs, fish, chicken), healthy fats (nuts, avocado), low-sugar fruits. What to reduce: Refined sugar, white rice/bread, processed foods, sugary drinks. This diet reduces insulin resistance, which is the root driver of PCOS symptoms.
