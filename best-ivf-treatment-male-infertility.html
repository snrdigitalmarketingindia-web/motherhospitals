Why ICSI Changes Everything: In conventional IVF, hundreds of thousands of sperm compete to fertilise an egg — which means even moderate male factor infertility causes failure. ICSI eliminates this barrier entirely. One sperm, selected by the embryologist, injected directly into the egg. Male factor infertility becomes largely irrelevant to fertilisation success.

Conventional IVF vs ICSI — Which is Better for Male Infertility?

Conventional IVF

Standard IVF (no ICSI)

  • Suitable for normal semen parameters
  • Lower lab cost
  • Requires 100,000+ motile sperm
  • Not suitable for significant male factor
  • Fails with poor motility or morphology
  • High fertilisation failure rate with abnormal sperm
ICSI — Recommended for Male Infertility

ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection)

  • Works with even 1 sperm per egg
  • Motility irrelevant — embryologist injects sperm
  • Morphology less critical — best sperm selected
  • Used with TESA sperm (azoospermia)
  • Higher fertilisation rate than IVF for male factor
  • Gold standard for all male infertility cases

Mother Hospitals recommends ICSI for all cases with significant male factor infertility. The marginal additional lab cost of ICSI is offset by significantly higher fertilisation rates.

When is ICSI Recommended?

All of the following conditions are treated with ICSI at Mother Hospitals Hyderabad

📉
Oligospermia (Low Sperm Count)
Moderate to severe oligospermia (below 5 million/mL). ICSI selects the best available sperm — even with very few sperm, fertilisation rates remain excellent.
🚫
Azoospermia (No Sperm)
TESA retrieves sperm from the testis, which is then used in ICSI. Combined TESA+ICSI cycle is coordinated with egg collection from the female partner.
🐢
Asthenospermia (Poor Motility)
ICSI bypasses the need for sperm to swim. Even with 0% progressive motility (or immotile-but-alive sperm), ICSI achieves fertilisation by direct injection.
🔬
Teratospermia (Abnormal Shape)
Normal morphology below 4% by Kruger criteria. ICSI's embryologist selects the best-shaped available sperm, overcoming poor morphology in the majority of cases.
🧬
High DNA Fragmentation
PICSI (Physiological ICSI) selects sperm with lower DNA damage. For very high DFI, TESA sperm has 3–4x less damage than ejaculated sperm — dramatically improving embryo quality.
❌
Previous IVF Failure
If previous conventional IVF cycles failed due to poor fertilisation or embryo quality — ICSI with or without PICSI/TESA can overcome the barriers that caused prior failure.

The IVF + ICSI Process at Mother Hospitals

  • 1

    Male Fertility Assessment

    Semen analysis, hormone panel, DNA fragmentation test, scrotal ultrasound. All results explained. Treatment protocol designed around your specific parameters.

  • 2

    Female Partner Stimulation

    The female partner is given hormone injections (7–14 days) to stimulate multiple egg development. Regular monitoring with ultrasound and blood tests. Triggered when follicles are ready.

  • 3

    Sperm Collection (Same Day as Egg Retrieval)

    Semen sample collected on the day of egg retrieval. For azoospermia: TESA performed on the same day under local anaesthesia — sperm retrieved directly from the testis and prepared for ICSI.

  • 4

    ICSI — Fertilisation

    Embryologist selects a single sperm (or TESA sperm) and injects it directly into each mature egg under high magnification. PICSI used for high DFI cases. 60–80% fertilisation rate per mature egg.

  • 5

    Embryo Culture (5–6 Days)

    Fertilised eggs develop in the lab to blastocyst stage (Day 5–6). Only good-quality blastocysts are transferred or frozen. PGT-A (genetic testing) available for high-risk cases or advanced age.

  • 6

    Embryo Transfer

    One or two blastocysts transferred to the uterus on Day 5–6. Extra embryos are cryopreserved for future frozen embryo transfer (FET) cycles. Pregnancy test 12 days after transfer.

Frequently Asked Questions — IVF for Male Infertility

What is the best IVF treatment for male infertility?
ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection) is the gold-standard IVF treatment for male infertility. Unlike conventional IVF, ICSI uses just one sperm per egg — injected directly by the embryologist. This makes it effective for low count, poor motility, abnormal morphology, and surgically retrieved sperm (TESA for azoospermia). Mother Hospitals Boduppal has completed 5,000+ ICSI cycles.
What is the difference between IVF and ICSI?
Conventional IVF: Sperm are placed with eggs in a dish — sperm must swim to fertilise. Requires 100,000+ good-quality sperm. ICSI: One sperm selected by the embryologist and injected directly into the egg. Requires only 1 sperm per egg. Works regardless of motility, morphology, or low count. ICSI is recommended for all significant male factor cases.
What is TESA and when is it used in IVF?
TESA (Testicular Sperm Aspiration) retrieves sperm from the testis using a fine needle under local anaesthesia — used when no sperm are in the ejaculate (azoospermia). Retrieved sperm is used in ICSI to fertilise eggs. 30-minute outpatient procedure at Mother Hospitals with minimal discomfort.
What is the IVF success rate for male infertility at Mother Hospitals?
Mother Hospitals reports 60–80% fertilisation rate per mature egg with ICSI. Overall IVF success rates depend on the female partner's age and egg quality. Dr. E. Prashanthi Reddy provides personalised success rate estimates at the initial consultation based on your specific parameters.
