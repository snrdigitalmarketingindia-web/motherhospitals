      Understanding PCOS

      India's most common hormonal disorder in women of reproductive age

      PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) affects 1 in 5 women in India. It is a hormonal disorder characterised by irregular periods, excess androgens (male hormones), and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound. Despite the name, "cysts" are not the main problem — it's the hormonal imbalance that causes the symptoms.

      Rotterdam Criteria for PCOS Diagnosis

      A formal PCOS diagnosis requires at least 2 of 3 Rotterdam criteria: (1) irregular or absent periods, (2) clinical or biochemical signs of hyperandrogenism (excess male hormones), and (3) polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound. Thyroid disease and high prolactin must first be excluded.

      PCOS and Fertility

      PCOS is the most common cause of ovulatory infertility, accounting for 70–80% of cases. However, with proper treatment, most women with PCOS can conceive. Ovulation induction with letrozole is highly effective, and IVF works well for PCOS patients when needed.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      How is PCOS diagnosed?

      PCOS is diagnosed using the Rotterdam criteria: at least 2 of 3 criteria must be present — (1) irregular or absent periods, (2) clinical or biochemical signs of excess androgens (facial hair, acne, elevated testosterone), and (3) polycystic ovaries on ultrasound (12+ small follicles or enlarged ovary >10mL). This screening quiz helps identify risk — formal diagnosis requires clinical evaluation.

      What are the most common PCOS symptoms in Indian women?

      In Indian women, the most common PCOS symptoms are: irregular or absent periods, weight gain especially around the abdomen, facial and body hair growth (hirsutism), acne that doesn't respond to usual treatments, hair thinning on the scalp, darkened skin patches around the neck (acanthosis nigricans), and difficulty conceiving.

      Can PCOS be cured?

      PCOS cannot be cured but can be effectively managed. Lifestyle changes (weight loss of even 5–10% can restore ovulation in many women), regular exercise, low-glycaemic index diet, and medications like metformin, clomiphene, or letrozole (for ovulation induction) are all proven treatments. Most women with PCOS can conceive with appropriate treatment.

      What tests are done to diagnose PCOS?

      Tests for PCOS include: pelvic ultrasound (to check ovarian morphology and antral follicle count), blood tests on Day 2–5 of cycle (FSH, LH, AMH, total testosterone, DHEAS, prolactin, TSH, fasting insulin, fasting glucose, HbA1c). AMH is often elevated in PCOS (>5 ng/mL) and is a useful marker.

      Can I get pregnant with PCOS?

      Yes — the majority of women with PCOS can conceive, either naturally with lifestyle changes or with fertility treatments. Ovulation induction with letrozole or clomiphene is first-line treatment. IVF is highly effective for PCOS if simpler treatments are unsuccessful. Dr. E. Prashanthi Reddy has helped thousands of women with PCOS achieve pregnancy at Mother Hospitals.

      Is PCOS related to insulin resistance?

      Yes — approximately 70% of women with PCOS have some degree of insulin resistance, regardless of body weight. Insulin resistance drives excess androgen production and disrupts ovulation. This is why metformin (an insulin-sensitising drug) and low-GI diets are core treatments for PCOS. All women with PCOS should have fasting insulin and glucose checked.
